## Memo (verbatim)

Meridian Fabrication is a precision contract manufacturer based in Columbus, Ohio — 312 employees, two facilities, has been with Centerline since 2019. Primary contact is David Kwan, CFO. His wife Michelle handles operations and joined our January call. The business has been family-controlled since founding in 2007. There's a minority silent partner who we've never met and who doesn't appear to be involved in day-to-day decisions.

The company makes machined aluminum and steel components — tight-tolerance work, automotive Tier 1 suppliers have been their bread and butter. Over the past 18 months they've been trying to diversify into aerospace subcontracting, which is the right long-term move, but the timing of that transition is now directly driving our credit concerns.

We have an $8.5MM facility — term loan plus revolver, variable rate SOFR+275. The loan was originally for equipment expansion and working capital when they were growing through the automotive cycle. Credit grade is B+. <!-- §2.1 RESTRICTED: internal credit rating --> Maturity is March 2027. Personal guarantee from David and Michelle Kwan.

The Q4 covenant package came in on January 18th — 18 days past the December 31 deadline, and no heads-up before it arrived. This is the second late package this year; Q2 was also late by 12 days. I've flagged the pattern to David but he hasn't offered a process fix. The late submission technically triggered a reporting default under our November waiver; the waiver deadline was January 31 and the package arrived before that, so the default was cured, but it was closer than I'd like.

DSCR for Q4 came in at 1.10x against our 1.20x floor. Leverage is at 4.1x against a 4.0x maximum. Both metrics have been moving the wrong direction all year — DSCR was 1.41x in January and leverage was 3.2x. The decline has been steady, not a single-event drop. Revenue was down about 13% for the year versus 2023, driven by automotive OEM softness. Their two largest automotive customers are running below prior-year production volumes and David says orders were down 8% YoY through Q3. EBITDA margins have compressed along with it — down from around 14% to 12% as their fixed cost base doesn't scale with the revenue decline. They've kept their headcount to stay ready for aerospace volume, which I understand strategically but it's compressing margins in the meantime.

The revolver situation worries me more than the DSCR. Utilization went from 38% at the start of 2024 to 69% by December. David has characterized this as seasonal working capital needs, but the trend is linear and persistent — there's nothing seasonal about it. They're using the revolver to cover operating cash flow shortfalls. At current trajectory that's a problem before the aerospace revenue inflection arrives, especially if the aerospace timeline slips again.

We granted a waiver on November 22nd covering the Q3 DSCR breach (1.18x, just under the 1.20x minimum) and anticipating the Q4 breach would continue. The conditions were: Q4 package by January 31 and updated projections showing a recovery path to covenant compliance by Q3 2025. The package arrived; the projections have not.

The aerospace contract is the thing I keep coming back to. When I first started flagging DSCR concerns in mid-2024, David told me the aerospace revenue would come through in Q2 2024 and resolve the shortfall. Then it was Q4 2024. Now his January cover letter says Q2 2025. Each time the explanation is "timing, not contract risk" — and that may be true, but we have asked twice for the underlying purchase order documentation and it hasn't arrived. I raised this on the January 23rd call with David and Marcus Chen. David said he'd send it. As of today I don't have it. I don't know if this is administrative slowness on David's part or if there's something about the contract status he's not fully disclosing. I want to give him the benefit of the doubt given the relationship, but I can't keep accepting verbal assurances on a recovery driver that's moved twice.

Marcus Chen asked on the call whether the aerospace PO was firm or conditional. David said "firm" but also said the first milestone was still contingent on their GC completing a tooling qualification. That answer felt evasive to me. I've asked for the document twice and I'm asking again here: we need to see the purchase order before we can support any continued waiver status.

On the AR aging — the Q4 package shows elevated 60-90 day balances and David mentioned two automotive customers went to extended payment terms at year-end. He called it seasonal but acknowledged it's more pronounced than prior years. I asked for the specific customer names on those AR items because if it's one of the Tier 1 suppliers we've seen struggling, that changes the receivable quality picture. Marcus flagged this on the internal review too. David hasn't provided the customer names yet; that's also still outstanding.

David Kwan has been a cooperative counterpart over six years. He doesn't hide bad news — he just delivers it late and often without the supporting documentation I need to act on it. The business itself is sound. They have long-standing customer relationships, their manufacturing quality is evidently good enough to win an aerospace subcontract, and the automotive headwind is real and sector-wide rather than company-specific. I don't think this is a management quality problem. I do think the credit has moved into territory where we need more information and more frequent visibility before we can assess whether the Q3 2025 recovery path is realistic or aspirational.

My read on the recovery timeline: if the aerospace contract delivers as now represented — first revenue Q2 2025, ramping through Q3 — and if the automotive segment stabilizes at current levels rather than declining further, DSCR could recover to 1.15-1.20x range by Q3. That's what they're projecting. But we've believed that projection twice before and it's moved twice. I want to see the documentation before I underwrite the recovery story again.

I am not recommending a second waiver at this time. I'm recommending the credit committee hold the existing waiver in place and set a February 14th deadline for David to deliver (1) the aerospace PO, (2) updated 12-month cash flow projections with assumptions stated, and (3) the names on the 60-90 day AR items. If those arrive and support the recovery narrative, I'll be back with a waiver extension recommendation. If they don't arrive by the 14th, I want to initiate the watchlist classification discussion. <!-- §2.1 RESTRICTED: watchlist classification -->

One more thing to flag: I want to restrict additional revolver draws above 75% utilization pending this review. We're at 69% now. I don't want them drawing the revolver to 85% while we're waiting on documentation that may change our view of the credit.

*Sarah Okonkwo — January 31, 2025*
