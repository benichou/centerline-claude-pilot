# Marcus Webb

**Title:** Relationship Manager, Commercial Banking · **Tenure:** 22 years
**Portfolio:** 71 active relationships | $189M in total commitments
**Specialty:** Retail, consumer services, hospitality, multi-unit operators

## How He Works
Marcus is the highest-producing RM on the team by total commitments. He has relationships that have been with Centerline for 15+ years. Several of his borrowers have his cell phone number and call him directly on weekends about things unrelated to banking. He is what the team calls a "classic banker."

His workflow is almost entirely relationship-driven. He does most of his documentation in short bursts immediately after client conversations — "while it's fresh." He uses Outlook for everything, Word for memos (no templates, writes from scratch), and Excel for his own tracking. He uses nCino under protest. "It takes me 15 clicks to do something I used to do in 3."

He arrives at the office at 8:30am and rarely checks email before that. He makes two to three client calls before 10am. Afternoons are for internal work. He leaves by 5:30pm consistently.

**His most time-consuming task:** "Honestly? The annual reviews. I have 71 relationships. That's 71 annual review memos. Some of them are straightforward but a lot of them require real thought."

His relationship with the credit team is occasionally tense. He believes credit analysts sometimes "get in the way of good banking." He has strong opinions about which covenant structures actually serve the bank's interests.

## Relationship with Technology
Marcus uses: Outlook, Word, Excel (basic). He does not use nCino more than required. He has not used any AI tools and has no intention of doing so voluntarily.

On AI: "I've heard the pitch. It's been the pitch for every new system for 20 years. The bank rolled out nCino to 'save us time' and it added two hours a week to my administrative burden. I'll believe it when I see it."

He is not hostile to the adoption program. He is indifferent to it in a way that could become hostile if the program feels like surveillance or adds compliance burden. "If it helps me, I'll use it. If it creates more things for me to explain to compliance, I won't touch it."

**His deepest concern** — which he has not stated explicitly but his manager is aware of: Marcus has built his value on relationship knowledge and institutional memory that is entirely in his head. He is 54 years old. He worries, without saying so, that AI tools will make newer, less experienced RMs look like they know as much as he does. "The system can see the data. It can't see 22 years of sitting across the table from these people."

**What would make him a user:** A single workflow that removes administrative burden from his annual review process without requiring him to change how he thinks about the work. He will not change his workflow for a tool. The tool has to fit his workflow.

**What would make him an active resister:** Any perception that the system is tracking his contact frequency and reporting it to management. "I have 71 relationships. I know which ones need attention and which ones don't. I've been doing this for 22 years. I don't need a computer to tell me I haven't called Summit in three weeks."

**His hidden leverage:** Marcus mentors four junior RMs informally. If he adopts something, they all will. If he publicly dismisses it, some of them will too.
